Lines and Angles

1. Introduction to Lines

A line is a straight one-dimensional figure that extends infinitely in both directions. It is defined by two points on a plane and has no thickness.

Types of Lines:

  • Line: A straight path that extends endlessly in both directions.
  • Line Segment: A part of a line that has two definite endpoints.
  • Ray: A part of a line that starts from a fixed point and extends infinitely in one direction.
  • Parallel Lines: Lines that are equidistant from each other and never meet, no matter how far they are extended.
  • Perpendicular Lines: Two lines that intersect at a right angle (90°).

2. Angles

An angle is formed when two rays meet at a common point called the vertex. The measure of an angle is given in degrees (°).

Types of Angles:

  • Acute Angle: An angle that measures less than 90°.
  • Right Angle: An angle that measures exactly 90°.
  • Obtuse Angle: An angle that measures more than 90° but less than 180°.
  • Straight Angle: An angle that measures exactly 180°, forming a straight line.
  • Reflex Angle: An angle that measures more than 180° but less than 360°.
  • Complete Angle: An angle that measures exactly 360°, forming a full circle.

3. Pairs of Angles

When two lines intersect, different pairs of angles are formed. These include:

  • Complementary Angles: Two angles whose measures add up to 90°.
  • Supplementary Angles: Two angles whose measures add up to 180°.
  • Adjacent Angles: Two angles that have a common vertex and a common side, but do not overlap.
  • Vertically Opposite Angles: Angles formed when two lines intersect. Vertically opposite angles are always equal.

4. Transversal and Corresponding Angles

A transversal is a line that crosses two or more parallel lines. The angles formed between a transversal and parallel lines are called corresponding angles.
For example:

  • Corresponding Angles: Angles that occupy the same relative position at each intersection where a transversal crosses two parallel lines. Corresponding angles are always equal.
  • Alternate Interior Angles: Angles on opposite sides of the transversal but inside the parallel lines. These angles are always equal.
  • Alternate Exterior Angles: Angles on opposite sides of the transversal but outside the parallel lines. These angles are also equal.
  • Co-interior Angles: Angles on the same side of the transversal and inside the parallel lines. These angles add up to 180°.

5. Measuring Angles

Angles are measured in degrees (°) using a protractor.

  • To measure an angle, align the base of the protractor with one side of the angle and read the number on the protractor where the other side intersects.
  • Acute, obtuse, and reflex angles can be measured using the inner or outer scale of the protractor.
